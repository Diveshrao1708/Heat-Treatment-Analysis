HEAT TREATMENT

Introduction

Heat Treatment is the process of controlled heating and cooling of metals  to alter their mechanical properties without changing the product shape. This process can improve the hardness, strength, toughness, and ductility of steels, making them suitable for various applications.

Types  

There are several types of heat treatment processes used for mild steel, including:

  1. Annealing: Annealing involves heating the steel to a specific temperature and then slowly cooling it to room temperature. This process helps soften the steel and improve its machinability.
  2. Normalizing: Normalizing is similar to annealing but involves cooling the steel in air. This process helps refine the grain structure of the steel and improve its mechanical properties.
  3. Quenching: Quenching involves rapidly cooling the heated steel by immersing it in a quenching medium, such as water or oil. This process helps harden the steel by trapping carbon atoms in a supersaturated state.
  4. Tempering: Tempering involves reheating the quenched steel to a specific temperature and then cooling it slowly. This process helps reduce the hardness of the steel and improve its toughness and ductility.
  5. Martempering and Austempering: These are specialized forms of quenching and tempering processes that are used to achieve specific properties in the steel, such as high toughness and ductility, along with high strength.

TTT diagram

Applications:

  1. Hardening: Heat treatment can be used to harden steels, making them suitable for applications requiring high wear resistance, such as cutting tools, gears, and bearings.
  2. Softening: Heat treatment can also be used to soften steels, making them easier to machine or form into various shapes.
  3. Improving toughness: Heat treatment can improve the toughness of steels, making them less brittle and more resistant to impact loading. This is beneficial for components subjected to high impact loads, such as shafts and springs.
  4. Stress relief: Heat treatment can relieve internal stresses in steels, reducing the risk of distortion or cracking in the material. This is particularly important for components that have been heavily machined or welded.

Limitations:

  1. Distortion: One of the main limitations of heat treatment is the risk of distortion in the material. Uneven heating or cooling can cause the material to warp or bend, leading to dimensional inaccuracies.
  2. Cracking: Rapid cooling during quenching can sometimes lead to cracking in the material, particularly for steels with high carbon content. Care must be taken to control the cooling rate to avoid this issue.
  3. Brittleness: In some cases, heat treatment can increase the brittleness of steels, particularly if they are quenched and tempered to a high hardness. This can reduce the toughness of the material, making it more susceptible to cracking under impact loading.
  4. Preparation: The polished sample is cleaned and dried before etching to ensure that the etchant can penetrate the microstructure evenly.

Grain Size Measurement

PLANIMETRIC METHOD

The planimetric method for grain size measurement involves measuring the size of individual grains in a microstructure using a microscope. To use the planimetric method, a sample of the material is first prepared and polished to a mirror-like finish. The sample is then etched to reveal the microstructure, which can be observed using a microscope. A photomicrograph is taken of the microstructure, and the grains are traced with a planimeter. The planimeter is used to trace the perimeter of each grain, and the total area of all the grains is measured. The number of grains measured is counted, and the average grain size is calculated using the following equation:

 d = 2(A_G / n)

 Where:

 d is the average grain size

 A_G is the total area of all the grains measured

 n is the number of grains measured

VICKERS HARDNESS TEST

Method

We used Vickers Hardness Test. The basic principle is to observe a material's ability to resist plastic deformation from a standard source. The Vickers test can be used for all metals and has one of the widest scales among hardness tests. The unit of hardness given by the test is known as the Vickers Pyramid Number (HV) or Diamond Pyramid Hardness (DPH). In our lab we used a ZwickRoell Vickers Hardness tester. It uses a diamond indenter to make an indentation which is measured and converted to a hardness value. A square base pyramid shaped diamond indenter is used for testing .

Vickers hardness, a measure of the hardness of a material, calculated from the size of an impression produced under load by a pyramid-shaped diamond indenter. It was decided that the indenter shape should be capable of producing geometrically similar impressions, irrespective of size; the impression should have well-defined points of measurement; and the indenter should have high resistance to self-deformation. A diamond in the form of a square-based pyramid satisfied these conditions. It had been established that the ideal size of a Brinell impression was 3⁄8 of the ball diameter. As two tangents to the circle at the ends of a chord 3d/8 long intersect at 136°, it was decided to use this as the HEAT TREATMENT AND HARDNESS 6 included angle between plane faces of the indenter tip. This gives an angle from each face normal to the horizontal plane normal of 22° on each side. The angle was varied experimentally and it was found that the hardness value obtained on a homogeneous piece of material remained constant, irrespective of load. Accordingly, loads of various magnitudes are applied to a flat surface, depending on the hardness of the material to be measured. The HV number is then determined by the ratio F/A, where F is the force applied to the diamond in kilograms-force and A is the surface area of the resulting indentation in square millimeters.

Formulae:

The Vickers Hardness Number (VHN) is calculated using the formula:

 VHN = (1.854xF) / d²,
